# Commercial Fiction Best Practices

## Core Characteristics

Commercial fiction prioritizes:
- **Engaging, accessible storytelling**
- **Strong plot and pacing**
- **Clear character goals and stakes**
- **Reader entertainment and satisfaction**
- **Broad appeal** across demographics
- **Page-turning quality**

## Premise Development (Commercial)

### Focus Areas
- **High-concept hooks** that sell the story
- **Clear, compelling conflict**
- **Universal stakes** readers can relate to
- **Genre-appropriate elements**
- **Marketable logline** (one sentence pitch)

### Hook Patterns
- "What if [interesting situation]?"
- Ordinary person in extraordinary circumstances
- High stakes from page one
- Relatable protagonist with clear goal
- Timely or trending themes

## Outline Structure (Commercial)

### Preferred Approaches
- **Three-act structure** (proven and reliable)
- **Save the Cat beat sheet** (precise plotting)
- **Clear escalation** of conflict and stakes
- **Satisfying payoffs** for all setup
- **Page-turning chapter endings**

### Beat Sheet Application
- Hit every major beat **clearly and strongly**
- Midpoint should be a **major twist or reversal**
- "All is Lost" is genuinely **devastating**
- Climax is **exciting, high-stakes confrontation**
- Resolution **satisfies reader expectations**

### Pacing Targets
- **Hook in first pages** (inciting incident by 10-15%)
- **End of Act I** around 25% (commitment)
- **Midpoint** at 50% (game-changer)
- **All is Lost** around 75%
- **Climax** in final 10-15%

## Prose Style (Commercial)

### Language Characteristics
- **Clear, accessible** prose
- **Active voice** predominates
- **Varied but not complex** sentence structures
- **Strong verbs** and concrete nouns
- **Minimize adverbs and adjectives**
- **Fast-paced when needed**, contemplative when earned

### Techniques to Embrace
- Strong openings (hook immediately)
- Cliffhanger chapter endings
- Scene-sequel structure
- Clear cause and effect
- Tension in every scene
- Emotional beats that land

### Things to Avoid
- Purple prose or overwrought description
- Slow starts or info dumps
- Passive voice
- Unclear action or stakes
- Meandering subplots
- Self-indulgent asides

## Character Development (Commercial)

### Focus
- **Relatable, sympathetic** protagonists
- **Clear wants and needs**
- **Active characters** who drive plot
- **Distinct, memorable** supporting cast
- **Character arcs that satisfy**

### Protagonist Traits
- Likable or at least compelling
- Has agency (makes choices that matter)
- Faces meaningful obstacles
- Grows/changes by end
- Has clear strengths and flaws

### Arc Patterns
- Clear transformation (flat arc for some genres)
- Learns lesson that helps them win
- Overcomes flaw to achieve goal
- Earns their happy (or appropriate) ending

## Pacing and Structure (Commercial)

### Pacing Principles
- **No sagging middle** - maintain momentum
- **Escalating stakes** throughout
- **Multiple plot threads** for variety
- **Ticking clocks** create urgency
- **Disaster-reaction-dilemma** cycles

### Scene Construction
- Every scene has **clear goal and obstacle**
- Scenes **advance plot or deepen character**
- **Strong scene openings and endings**
- **Vary scene length** for rhythm
- **Cut late, leave early** (in medias res)

### Chapter Structure
- **Strong chapter openings** (hook reader back in)
- **Cliffhangers or questions** at chapter end
- **Vary chapter length** for pacing control
- **Multiple POVs** can amp excitement
- **End chapters on high notes** or mysteries

## Dialogue (Commercial)

### Characteristics
- **Snappy, purposeful** exchanges
- **Reveals character** efficiently
- **Advances plot** through information sharing
- **Contains subtext** but not obscure
- **Avoids "on the nose"** exposition
- **Realistic but edited** (remove filler)

### Techniques
- Tag only when needed for clarity
- Action beats between dialogue
- Distinct voices per character
- Conflict or tension in conversations
- Witty banter when appropriate
- Emotional truth in key moments

## World-Building (Commercial)

### Approach
- **Establish quickly** without info dumps
- **Sensory details** that ground reader
- **Rule of three** for descriptions
- **Show through action** not exposition
- **Only include what's relevant** to story
- **Make setting a character** when appropriate

### Genre Considerations
- **Genre-appropriate detail level**
  - Thriller: procedural accuracy
  - Fantasy: magic system clarity
  - Romance: emotional atmosphere
  - Sci-fi: technology believability

## Plot Structure (Commercial)

### Essential Elements
- **Inciting incident** disrupts status quo
- **Rising action** with escalating complications
- **Midpoint shift** changes everything
- **Dark night** tests protagonist
- **Climax** brings all threads together
- **Resolution** satisfies and surprises

### Subplot Guidelines
- **2-3 subplots** maximum
- Each subplot **supports main plot or theme**
- Subplots **resolve before climax**
- Romance subplot (common across genres)
- B-story carries theme

## Reader Expectations (Commercial)

### What Commercial Readers Expect
- **Engaging story** from page one
- **Clear protagonist to root for**
- **Escalating conflict and stakes**
- **Emotional investment**
- **Satisfying conclusion**
- **Genre promises delivered**

### Pacing Expectations
- No slow spots
- Constant forward momentum
- Tension in every scene
- Regular plot developments
- Payoffs for patience

### Ending Expectations
- **HEA (Happily Ever After)** for romance
- **Justice/victory** for thriller/mystery
- **Hope** even in darker stories
- **Emotional satisfaction**
- **Loose ends tied up**

## Genre-Specific Considerations

### Romance Commercial
- Meet-cute early
- Believable obstacles
- Chemistry on page
- HEA/HFN required

### Thriller/Mystery Commercial
- High stakes from start
- Clues and red herrings
- Ticking clock pressure
- Satisfying reveal

### Fantasy/Sci-Fi Commercial
- Clear magic/tech rules
- Worldbuilding through story
- Epic scope with personal stakes
- Originality within tropes

## Market Considerations (Commercial)

### Writing for the Market
- **Know your genre** and its conventions
- **Study bestsellers** in your category
- **Understand reader expectations**
- **Balance originality with familiarity**
- **Write to word count** (genre-specific)
- **Consider series potential**

### Comp Titles Approach
- Identify 2-3 comparable books
- Note what made them successful
- Find the gap you can fill
- Deliver on their promise with your twist
