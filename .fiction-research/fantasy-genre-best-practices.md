# Fantasy Fiction Best Practices

## Core Genre Elements

### Essential Components
- **Magic system** with clear rules and costs
- **Worldbuilding** that feels lived-in and coherent
- **Epic or personal stakes** (or both)
- **Wonder and sense of discovery**
- **Good vs. evil** (or nuanced morality)
- **Hero's journey** or chosen one tropes (or subversion)

## Premise Development (Fantasy)

### Hook Patterns
- Ordinary person discovers extraordinary power
- Hidden magical world revealed
- Quest to save world/kingdom/loved one
- Prophesied hero must fulfill destiny
- Political intrigue in fantasy setting
- Magic gone wrong or mysterious threat

### Stakes Levels
- **Personal**: Character's identity, freedom, loved ones
- **Local**: Village, city, or kingdom
- **Global**: Entire world or realm
- **Cosmic**: Balance of magic, multiple worlds

## Worldbuilding (Fantasy-Specific)

### Critical Elements
1. **Magic System**
   - How magic works (rules, limitations)
   - Who can use it and why
   - Costs or consequences of use
   - Cultural attitudes toward magic
   - How it affects society and technology

2. **Geography and Setting**
   - Map-worthy locations
   - Varied terrain and climates
   - Strategic importance of places
   - Journey potential

3. **History and Mythology**
   - Ancient conflicts or events
   - Lost civilizations or magic
   - Prophecies and legends
   - Historical cycles or patterns

4. **Cultures and Societies**
   - Distinct races/peoples with unique traits
   - Political systems and conflicts
   - Religious or magical beliefs
   - Social hierarchies
   - Cultural practices and taboos

5. **Economy and Daily Life**
   - How people make a living
   - Trade and commerce
   - Impact of magic on daily life
   - Technology level and why

### Worldbuilding Integration
- **Reveal through character experience** not exposition
- **Use all senses** to make world tangible
- **Show cultural differences** through details
- **Make world affect plot** (not just backdrop)
- **Iceberg principle**: know 10x more than you show

## Character Archetypes (Fantasy)

### Common Archetypes (To Use or Subvert)
- **The Chosen One**: Prophesied hero with destiny
- **The Mentor**: Wise guide (often dies mid-story)
- **The Warrior**: Skilled fighter with honor code
- **The Thief/Rogue**: Cunning, morally flexible
- **The Mage**: Powerful but often flawed magic user
- **The Noble**: Royal or aristocrat with duty conflicts
- **The Reluctant Hero**: Doesn't want the quest
- **The Dark Lord**: Embodiment of evil (or complex villain)

### Modern Fantasy Characters
- Flawed, complex protagonists
- Morally grey characters
- Strong female warriors (not just love interests)
- Found family dynamics
- LGBTQ+ characters integrated naturally
- Diverse cultural representation

## Plot Structure (Fantasy)

### Classic Fantasy Structure
1. **Ordinary World**: Before adventure begins
2. **Call to Adventure**: Quest, threat, or discovery
3. **Refusal/Reluctance**: Why hero resists
4. **Mentor Appears**: Guide provides tools/knowledge
5. **Crossing Threshold**: Leaving known world
6. **Tests and Trials**: Adventures in magical world
7. **Approach to Cave**: Preparing for major challenge
8. **Ordeal**: Near-death or major sacrifice
9. **Reward**: New power, knowledge, or item
10. **Road Back**: Return journey with complications
11. **Resurrection**: Final test using all learned
12. **Return with Elixir**: Changed hero, changed world

### Modern Variations
- Start in medias res (skip long setup)
- Subvert chosen one trope
- Political intrigue over quest
- Character-driven over plot-driven
- Ambiguous morality and outcomes

## Fantasy Subgenres

### High Fantasy (Epic)
- Secondary world (not Earth)
- Grand scale conflicts
- Good vs. Evil themes
- Multiple POV characters
- Series-oriented (trilogies+)
- Emphasis on worldbuilding

### Low Fantasy
- Real world with magical elements
- Personal stakes over world-saving
- Magic is rare or hidden
- Contemporary or historical setting
- Often standalone novels

### Dark Fantasy
- Morally ambiguous characters
- Horror elements (gore, dread)
- Grimdark worldview
- Anti-heroes protagonist
- Realistic violence consequences
- Political complexity

### Urban Fantasy
- Contemporary city setting
- Magic hidden in modern world
- Often first-person POV
- Mystery or thriller pacing
- Supernatural creatures
- Series potential

### Sword and Sorcery
- Action-oriented adventure
- Roguish protagonists
- Personal quests over saving world
- Pulp-inspired tone
- Exotic locales
- Less worldbuilding detail

## Magic Systems

### Hard Magic (Sanderson's Laws)
- **Clear rules** reader can understand
- **Limitations and costs** create tension
- **Problem-solving** with magic in climax
- Examples: Mistborn, Harry Potter wands

### Soft Magic
- **Mysterious and wondrous**
- **Unexplained or inconsistent**
- **Cannot solve main plot** (deus ex machina risk)
- **Creates atmosphere** and sense of other
- Examples: Lord of the Rings, Earthsea

### Cost/Limitation Types
- Physical exhaustion
- Life force or aging
- Mental strain or madness
- Rare materials/components
- Time or ritual requirements
- Moral corruption
- Environmental effects

## Pacing (Fantasy-Specific)

### Balance Needed
- **Action/adventure** vs. **character development**
- **Worldbuilding** vs. **plot momentum**
- **Political intrigue** vs. **personal stakes**
- **Description** vs. **forward motion**

### Chapter Rhythm
- Alternate action and quieter chapters
- Vary POV for fresh perspectives
- End chapters on hooks or questions
- Travel montages when journey not crucial
- Training sequences kept brief unless plot-relevant

## Dialogue (Fantasy)

### Considerations
- **Avoid faux-medieval "thee/thou"** (unless culturally specific)
- **Modern accessibility** with period flavor
- **Cultural differences** in speech patterns
- **Magic terminology** used naturally
- **Class distinctions** in language
- **Avoid infodumping** through dialogue

### Naming Conventions
- Consistent naming patterns per culture
- Pronounceable names (unless alienness is the point)
- Avoid apostrophes overload
- Match names to cultural inspirations
- Character names distinct from each other

## Common Fantasy Tropes

### Tropes to Use Thoughtfully
- Prophecies and chosen ones
- Magic swords/artifacts
- Dark lords and minions
- Ancient evil awakening
- Lost heir to throne
- Dragon riders/tamers
- Elves, dwarves, orcs (traditional races)

### Tropes to Avoid or Subvert
- Perfect heroes with no flaws
- Women only as love interests
- Monolithic "evil races"
- Medieval Europe as only setting
- Info dump prologues
- Deus ex machina endings
- Love triangle clichés

## Reader Expectations (Fantasy)

### What Fantasy Readers Expect
- **Immersive worldbuilding**
- **Sense of wonder** and discovery
- **Magic or supernatural elements**
- **Epic or meaningful stakes**
- **Character growth** and heroism
- **Good storytelling** over everything

### Subgenre-Specific Expectations
- **Epic**: Multiple books, grand scope
- **Grimdark**: No one is safe, moral complexity
- **Urban**: Modern setting, series potential
- **Romance**: HEA with fantasy elements

### What Will Disappoint
- Inconsistent magic rules
- Deus ex machina endings
- Flat worldbuilding
- Derivative clichés without fresh spin
- Plot holes in logic
- Unearned victories
